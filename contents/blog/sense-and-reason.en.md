## Earth, and the optimization algorithm

Evolution, or honestly the whole sweep of history, is really one long and chaotic optimization problem being solved out loud. Fitness is the **optimization goal**, and living things are the **function being optimized**.

There's a moment in *The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy* that has become a classic of science fiction:

> What is the ultimate answer to life, the universe, and everything?
>
> The supercomputer Deep Thought runs for seven and a half million years and finally delivers its answer: **42**.
>
> What does that mean?
>
> Deep Thought admits it hasn't yet found the real question that the answer belongs to.
>
> So, to compute that question, Deep Thought and the mice build an even greater computer called Earth, and the living things on Earth are part of the machine. The program runs for ten million years, and with just five minutes left to go, the Vogons demolish the planet to make way for a hyperspace bypass.

The first time I heard this, I was floored by the absurd, epic deadpan of Douglas Adams. The ultimate answer to the universe is a meaningless number, and Earth turns out to be a computer built to explain that number. It's that gorgeous sense of misplacement that lets Adams take a serious, enormous question and toss it to the reader as a joke. The man was good.

But the longer I chewed on it, the more "Earth is a computer" started to sound like plain truth. Isn't it, though? Earth set up an initial environment and spent billions of years running a genetic algorithm (cyanobacteria have been around for 2.1 billion years, which is far longer than Adams's mere ten million), searching for the "fittest" life, looking for the **optimal solution** among organisms that natural selection lets sustain and copy themselves. The good news: across 4.5 billion years of computation since the planet was born, our species, *Homo sapiens*, you and me, probably counts as a local optimum.

## What is reason?

Seen this way, a "rational decision by an organism" can be defined as "a survival strategy with high fitness." At first glance that seems fine, but it's completely different from how we usually talk about "reason." It might even contradict it.

Here's an extreme example.

* Random behavior is actually a fairly good survival strategy. In a wildly volatile, frankly hostile environment, randomness keeps fitness steady, and once the population is large enough, a random strategy lets at least some individuals survive even the worst conditions. (Think of viruses. Their survival strategy is close to pure randomness: copy like mad, mutate at a high rate, take a random walk. That's exactly why, faced with a targeted drug, some individuals will mutate their way into resistance and keep rendering vaccines and antibodies useless.)
* The reverse is also true. A delicate strategy exquisitely tuned to one specific environment has almost no resilience to risk. The giant panda's food supply is absurdly narrow, so the species is hypersensitive to change, and the moment its ecosystem is polluted it teeters on the edge of extinction.

On that measure, the virus is a far "better" organism than the panda. Its adaptability is so staggering that you can find it anywhere on Earth.

Another example is wheat. I came across this passage in Yuval Noah Harari's *Sapiens*: it was wheat that enslaved humans, wheat that used us to replicate itself by the billion and beat out every other plant in the struggle to survive.

> We did not domesticate wheat. It domesticated us.
>
> If we look at the Agricultural Revolution from the viewpoint of wheat, ten thousand years ago wheat was just one wild grass among many, growing in a small patch of the Middle East. Then, in the space of a thousand years, it suddenly spread across the world. Survival and reproduction are the basic standards of evolution, and by that standard wheat is one of the most successful plants in Earth's history. Take the Great Plains of North America: ten thousand years ago there was no wheat there at all, and now there are vast oceans of it rolling in the wind, with no other plant for hundreds of kilometers. Worldwide, wheat covers about 2.25 million square kilometers of the planet's surface, nearly ten times the size of Britain. How did this humble grass go from nothing to everywhere?
>
> The secret was that wheat manipulated *Homo sapiens* to its own ends.
>
> — Yuval Noah Harari, *Sapiens*

So we notice something: "reason" isn't quite the same thing as "intelligence" or "rationality," and "reason" itself isn't a tidy, unambiguous word. Asking whether an "organism's decisions" are rational and asking whether an individual creature's behavior is rational turn out to be two entangled but genuinely different questions.

To set aside the pointless word-splitting, let's just agree to call the "doesn't look very clever but works beautifully in the end" kind of strategy **low-level but rational**, and the "extremely complicated, extremely clever, and also turns out great" kind **high-level rational**, or **intelligent**.

With that out of the way, let's get into the big argument about "emotion" and "reason."

## Emotion is first-order reason

### Emotion and reason

We're forever telling each other to "let reason win over impulse," to "think about problems rationally." The implication is that **reason and rationality** are the good thing, the part that makes the right call, while **sensibility and emotion** are the bad thing, the part that makes us short-sighted, impulsive, irrational, and prone to worse decisions.

Is that really how it works?

I'd argue that what we call **emotion** is exactly the "low-level but rational" system I described above. Let me borrow a metaphor from Robert Pirsig's *Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance*:

> If you put an amoeba in a dish of water and drop a bit of dilute sulfuric acid near it, I think it would avoid the acid. Even though it has no idea what sulfuric acid is, if it could speak it would say, "This environment is bad." If it had a nervous system, it would go about overcoming the bad environment in far more complex ways. It would look for similar things from past experience, images or symbols, to "define" this unpleasant environment and so "understand" it.
>
> — Robert Pirsig, *Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance*

Set aside extreme forms of life like viruses (the field has long argued over whether a virus even counts as alive). A creature's first grasp of its environment is emotional. Pain, comfort, affection, disgust: these make up the instinct to seek what helps and flee what harms.

Pirsig makes one mistake in this passage. He's so attached to his own idea of "reason" and "understanding" that he overlooks how emotion is itself a piece of reason. What the amoeba needs to flee the acid isn't "rationality" at all, it's something much closer to what we'd call "emotion." And yet, in another sense, Pirsig is right too, because in this scene the "emotion" is functioning as the amoeba's "reason."

The amoeba touches the acid molecules, a few simple chemical signals fire, and it curls up and retreats. That is at once the "reason" that judges the outside world and the "emotion" that registers pain, panic, and revulsion. Here reason and emotion are one and the same, because emotion just is the form first-order reason takes.

So this is what I mean: **emotion** is a **first-order reasoning system**.

### How does emotion work?

> When a system is working, don't disturb it. Build on it instead. In natural systems, improvement means "patching" an existing, debugged system. The original layers keep running, and they may not even notice (or need to notice) that a new layer has been built on top of them.
>
> — Kevin Kelly, *Out of Control*

In *Out of Control*, Kevin Kelly lays out a general rule for complex systems, and I'll restate it more bluntly here: as long as some lower-level system works well, the higher layers won't tear it up. They'll just call its API and build the upper system on top of it.

According to existing work in psychology and neuroscience, humans share five primal emotions: fear, sadness, joy, anger, and disgust. With fMRI (functional magnetic resonance imaging) we can roughly map each emotion onto a corresponding brain region.

I want to borrow a term from programming, **encapsulation**, to describe emotion. The primal emotions encapsulate whole sets of situations along with the behaviors that fit them, so emotion can sit between "sensation" (the input) and "behavior" (the output) as a kind of go-between, an algorithm that helps the creature adapt.

There's more. Emotion also comes with learning and fuzzy pattern recognition. A child does something wrong and gets a swat on the bottom, and next time a similar situation comes up, fear shows up first, and the child steers clear of the same mistake. Our emotions run so well because they hold a complex, finely built challenge of dynamic learning, plus internal mechanisms that pull against each other (in a dangerous jungle there's also sweet fruit, so joy and fear start to compete, and emotion as an algorithm sets about solving this "multi-objective nonlinear programming problem"). A creature with the "emotion" algorithm baked in is extraordinarily good at adapting, and we read that as a rise in its "reason."

It's no exaggeration to say that if we could load emotion into a robot, we'd have a hard time telling it apart from a quiet, living person. It can't reason, can't read or write, but it feels pain, it weeps, it flinches away, it likes things clean, it stands up when it sees something unjust, it offers comfort out of empathy, and maybe, just maybe, it falls in love, it grows fond of looking up at the stars at night.

## Reason is self-referential emotion

### What on earth is "self-reference"?

Seeing that heading, you're surely about to ask: what does "self-reference" mean? Why has this clunky, vaguely impressive word suddenly appeared?

~~Relax. First go buy a copy of *Gödel, Escher, Bach*, and read it cover to cover with a reverent and curious heart...~~

"Self-reference," short for self-referentiality, is a concept from logic. In plain terms, it's something that contains itself.

**"This proposition cannot be proven"** is a classic self-referential statement. You can also call it a higher-order proposition, or the original proposition.

Sit with it for a while and it gets interesting:

* If the proposition can be proven, you get a contradiction.
* If the proposition cannot be proven, then the proposition is true.

In other words, with that single sentence I've constructed a "truth that cannot be proven." That's the charm of self-reference.

In fact, "this proposition cannot be proven" is a stripped-down version of Gödel's incompleteness theorem.

> **Gödel's incompleteness theorem**
>
> For any formal system that includes a basic description of elementary arithmetic and is consistent (self-consistent), there must exist propositions that the methods allowed within the system can neither prove true nor prove false.

Gödel's incompleteness theorem is famous. It reveals that within any mathematical system there are true propositions we can never prove, and it shattered Hilbert's dream of building a complete, fully axiomatized foundation for mathematics.

You've probably heard of the celebrated incompleteness theorem. But did you know there's a paired theorem, Gödel's *completeness* theorem?

If we forbid self-reference in a formal system and allow only first-order calculus, we get:

> **Gödel's completeness theorem**
>
> In first-order predicate calculus, every logically valid formula is provable.

My statements and arguments for these two theorems are not rigorous, of course. A full proof involves serious mathematics (the ingenious construction of "Gödel numbers," for one), and given the length here, forgive me for not unpacking the proofs of these two famous theorems.

Working through completeness and incompleteness, we come to see how central self-reference is in a formal system, and we catch a glimpse of its almost alchemical power to turn the ordinary into something extraordinary.

### Rational decisions and the somatic marker hypothesis

> ...But before you bring any cost/benefit analysis to bear on the problem, and before you reason toward the solution, something important happens: when a bad outcome connected with a given response option comes into mind, however fleetingly, you experience an unpleasant gut feeling. Because the feeling is about the body, I gave the phenomenon the technical term **somatic state**; and because it "marks" an image, I call it a **marker**. Note that I use somatic in the broadest sense, so that when I speak of somatic markers I mean both visceral and nonvisceral feelings.
>
> So how do somatic markers work? They force attention on the negative outcome to which a given action may lead, and they sound a warning: beware of the danger ahead if you choose this option. The signal may lead you to reject, immediately, the option that would lead to bad outcomes. This automatic signal protects you against future losses, and lets you then choose from among fewer alternatives. There is still room for using a proper cost/benefit analysis and proper deductive competence, but only after the somatic marker has narrowed the field.
>
> In short, the **somatic marker** is a special case of feelings generated from secondary emotions. Through learning, those emotions and feelings have been connected to predicted future outcomes of particular scenarios.
>
> — Antonio Damasio, *Descartes' Error*

The neuroscientist Antonio Damasio explains the neural basis of feeling and emotion in *Descartes' Error*. As a work of neuroscience from 1995, a fair amount of its viewpoints and methods look dated now, but the ideas and the questions it raises are still genuinely illuminating.

The "somatic marker" he describes is a refreshing explanation, and right now it's the account of "the neural mechanism behind judgment and intuition" that I find most convincing.

Over the course of evolution, old systems don't get abolished. They become the foundation for new ones, reused again and again. We don't have to reinvent the wheel. When we need it, we just call it again.

The **somatic marker hypothesis** is exactly this kind of theory. It says that a negative experience produces a negative bodily feeling (a negative emotion) and leaves behind a somatic marker. If a similar situation comes up later, the marker gets called again, so the higher system "borrows" the emotional system and hands us a bad feeling in advance, which then helps us decide. The same machinery runs in the other direction for positive feelings too.

The somatic marker theory explains a lot about real life. When you look up your exam scores, when you sit on the Ministry of Education website deciding which university to put down, when you decide to buy some expensive luxury or gadget, when you decide to pour a large sum into the stock market, when you decide to spend a fortune on a car or a house, your breathing tightens, your hand trembles, and in the instant before you press the button, your biological instinct, that ancient neural machinery that helped your ancestors dodge lions, is sounding the alarm: "Huge risk! Run!" It's because of this very mechanism that, facing the big choices with long shadows, our emotions keep us cautious and steer us away from risk.

On the whole, the idea that emotion does more harm than good for rational decisions is wrong. Our good decisions lean not only on rational analysis but on emotional experience.

### Imagining a life with sensibility stripped away

In *The Divine Comedy*, Dante lays out the seven deadly sins we all know: pride, envy, wrath, sloth, greed, gluttony, and lust.

In the much-loved anime *Fullmetal Alchemist: Brotherhood*, the great villain "Father" tries to split these seven temperaments out of his own body, to kill off his feelings. He believes this will purge his original sin, let him pin truth down inside himself, and so ascend to godhood.

I used to imagine the same thing. If I could peel my feelings away entirely and become a cold rational machine when making decisions, weighing pros and cons, ruling with pure logic, would my life get better? Would I make the best possible choices?

We're raised on it: be more rational, be more level-headed, set personal feeling aside, see the larger good clearly.

But does straining to suppress sensibility and lean wholly on reason really lead to better decisions?

I can't answer that, because the question itself is wrong.

There is no way to discuss reason while throwing sensibility away. Sensibility isn't something you can **discard**. Just the opposite: it's the ground on which we become human, the cornerstone of reason, and reason's purpose too.

### Self-reference, reason, and free will

"What is reason?" is a question that has puzzled me for years, and I'm still not ready to answer it.

In *Gödel, Escher, Bach*, Douglas Hofstadter offers a conjecture: intelligence arises from self-reference.

If you imagine a kind of "emotional awareness of emotion," an "emotion born from emotion," could that awareness be what we call reason?

To answer that, we'd inevitably crash into the walls of linguistics and semiotics. After all, it's hard to picture a "thought without language," and the things we've talked about today don't yet have a way to fold language into the framework.
