---

As words and as symbols, "Analog" and "Digital" sit far enough apart that the difference is worth taking seriously.

**"Analog"** means "a mapping from one continuous change to another continuous change." It is infinitely precise, dense with detail, messy and organic, fluid and felt.

**"Digital"** means, well, digital, numeric. It refers to other things "in a discrete, finite way." It is clearly defined, unambiguous, deterministic, computable.

- Analog is an isomorphic transfer between different carriers. Digital belongs to no carrier at all.

- Analog can be refined and described without end. The description of Digital is finite.

- Analog is informal. Digital is formal.

A few examples of the distinction:

### Sound

Vinyl records and old-fashioned radio broadcasts are Analog. A digital audio file is Digital.

The principle behind a record is to carve the continuous sound waves in the air into the grooves of the disc. Radio works in a similar way: sound waves are continuously turned into electromagnetic signals, and on the receiving end the electromagnetic signals are continuously restored into sound.

A digital audio file is something different in kind. We cannot store infinitely fine sound, because that would take infinite storage, so we are forced to make trade-offs.

For a digital audio file we have bit depth (how many possible "pitch" steps we can have, from the quietest sound to the loudest) and sampling rate (how many samples per unit of time we pull out of the continuous signal to build a discrete one). Any pitch lying between two smallest pitch steps, and any change in the wave inside the smallest interval of time, has to be thrown away, rounded off, before the file can live in digital memory.

For more on this, listen to the podcast *Off the Scene* and its episode [*S2E1 MP3*](https://www.xiaoyuzhoufm.com/episode/60c5f47cc2ec9bd456ec633b).

### Computers

There are two kinds of computer in the world: the analog computer and the digital computer.

To work out where a planet will be a year from now, you could use an orrery to imitate the planet's orbit, then crank a handle to spin the model and "speed up time" until you reach the future position. That is the Analog way of thinking.

In the early days of the digital computer, analog computers were already doing differentiation and integration with precise gear trains. They were used to predict eclipses and tides, and to guide anti-aircraft weapons. But an analog computer cannot be programmed. It can only carry out the intent it was built with. Mass production, miniaturization, and commercialization were all very hard, so the digital computer overtook it quickly.

When we say "computer" now, we always mean the digital kind. But something is shifting. Training a neural network leans on enormous amounts of matrix multiplication, and a traditional von Neumann architecture (the digital computer) runs that fairly inefficiently. The arrival of analog microelectronic devices that use analog signals (anywhere from 0 to 1) instead of digital ones (0 or 1), the so-called compute-in-memory devices, can cut energy use dramatically while doing the same job.

### Language

If I ask you "how tall is Mount Everest," and you happen to know, you will tell me it is 8,848 meters, or to be more exact, 8,848.86 meters. You cannot, and have no need to, tell me anything else. That is Digital.

But if I ask you "what is Mount Everest like," you will describe how it towers into the sky, how it wraps itself in cloud and mist, how it is pure and untouched, how it stands there, great and unmoving.

I can keep asking, "anything else? and then?", and you seem able to keep answering forever. That is Analog.

When Professor Chen Jiaying used this same example, he did not bring up the Analog and Digital distinction. In his book he uses it to talk about "perceptual speech" and "rational speech." To my mind, the pair of concepts he discusses there, perception and reason, has a strong resemblance to Analog and Digital.

For more, read Chen Jiaying, *Perception, Reason, Self-Knowledge*, Beijing: Beijing Daily Press, 2022, p. 126.

---

Analog and Digital make up a quiet, ambiguous kind of opposition. Trying to draw a clean boundary between them is itself a Digital act, so for an Analog thinker the two are tangled together, dependent on each other. One of the goals of digitalization is to simulate our real world, and analogy, in turn, can never quite escape the need to refer and to quantify.

This section is just about Digital.

In *37: A Vast Resemblance Linking All Things*, the host Baoting says:

> "The role Digital plays is exactly the role our fingers play."

Stretching that a little, I'd sort it into five jobs.

### Counting, quantifying, calculating

The most basic job of Digital. It needs little explanation.

### Pointing (reference, index, and pointer)

One of the most important differences between Digital and Analog is that Digital simplifies and abstracts whatever it points at.

We use GDP and broad market indices to point at the macroeconomic state of two great powers. We use numbers to stand in for every specific person who died in a war or a plague. Using less data, and more understandable data, to refer to something far more complex and tangled, stripping away the redundant detail, makes things easier to discuss and analyze and makes it easier to find patterns inside the chaos. That is the nature and the advantage of Digital.

### Comparison

A number carries comparison inside it from the start.

When I say a test scored 85, it is not a lonely 85. It is bound, of necessity, to "higher than 80" and "lower than 90." That is to say, a single grade is tied to a whole comparison system that judges people along one dimension.

When you happen to meet a young person in a café, someone who loves the world and is full of hope and confusion about the future, you would not judge them through that kind of system, because character and spark are "incomparable," Analog rather than Digital. But the moment you assign a person a "value," whether it's a height of 180, a salary of a million, an A+, none of it can shake off that faint tint of comparison.

When we say "the cold hard numbers," we look like we're after objectivity. What we're really after is comparison.

Digital cannot be separated from the ideology of meritocracy, and a society where everything can be set on the scale and weighed is anything but an ideal one.

### Manipulation and management

Put all of the above together and Digital can serve to manipulate and to manage. People often say we are now "controlled by numbers." This does not just mean "I am controlled by something, and that something happens to be a number." It means "it is precisely because numbers exist that I feel controlled." Exam scores, performance reviews, digital management, electronic files, price discrimination from big data, recommendation algorithms... the digital world forms one tight, complete system. It gave humans the ability to cooperate at massive scale, and it lets every one of us feel the violence that comes from that same system.

### Reasoning without feeling (the Turing machine)

Digital is also the engine driving the explosion of technology. The most important opening for the scientific revolution was not scientists boldly defying the Church. It was the founding of a tradition of scholarly critique and the mathematization of the natural sciences.

(A small aside: the heliocentric model had been proposed again and again ever since the Greeks, and even within the Church there were people who supported it. The Copernican revolution was not about proposing heliocentrism, and the scientific revolution did not happen as simply as the textbooks describe.)

Why does mathematization matter so much? Why is it that only after a field becomes mathematized does it earn the right to be called a "science"? Because mathematization makes "reasoning without feeling" possible. The arguments of philosophers (the rationalists and logicians aside) tend to be short. The concepts may be hard to grasp, the thought may be obscure and deep, but there is no "long-line reasoning," no tightly chained, airtight proof that goes on and on. In mathematical physics this is routine. Theory papers running dozens of pages of proof are everywhere.

For exactly this reason, the Greeks could work out that the Earth is round (through their grasp of plane geometry, and through watching the horizon and lunar eclipses), and could work out that the heavenly bodies move in circles, but they had no possibility at all of doing what modern physicists do: deriving neutron stars, black holes, the uncertainty principle, and quantum fluctuations out of equations. Mathematics is our only way of carrying out "long-line precise reasoning," letting our sight reach into the micro and macro universes that perception can never touch.

If you asked me what ability belongs to humans alone, I would not answer "language" or "intelligence." I would answer, carefully:

> Perhaps the capacity for Digital is something humans have and other animals do not.

Liu Cixin tells this story in his novella *Sermon on the Mount of the Dao*:

Just as humanity is about to run an experiment that would destroy the universe, an observer civilization, advanced as gods (the Hazard Removers), forcibly brings us to a stop. To keep civilizations from destroying the universe with creation-level experiments, the Hazard Removers have set early-warning systems all across the cosmos. A scientist asks them: "When did the warning system you placed on Earth first go off? The age of Newton? The age of Aristotle?"

The Hazard Remover answers mildly: "At the end of the Pleistocene, 270,000 years ago, a primitive human gazed up at the stars for longer than the warning threshold allowed, showing excessive curiosity about the universe. If those few minutes the primitive spent staring at the cosmos were the moment of seeing a gemstone, then your entire so-called human civilization that followed has been nothing more than bending down to pick it up."

I love *Sermon on the Mount of the Dao*. When Liu Cixin wrote it his prose was still a little green, but that does nothing to stop it from being, for me, one of the best short and medium science fiction stories there is. The romance of "gazing up at the stars" is something other images come nowhere near. And yet I feel the alarm going off the moment "a primitive counts on their fingers" would fit reality better. The arrival of digitalization let humanity, a small and fragile civilization that builds its walls out of river mud, on one little pebble in this vast cosmos, see into the secrets of the grand universe and the microscopic world armed with nothing but observation, experiment, and pen and paper.

Of course, Digital has its bad side too. As I said above, digitalization is not an ideology-free process. On the contrary, it carries a violence that comes from standardization, modernization, and scale, a structural, top-down alienation and domination of people. And that is exactly the space where Analog and the humanist spirit ought to step in. This touches on the friction between the two worlds that Analog and Digital span, and I'll save it for section 4.

Digital is more than a double-edged sword. It is more like a dimension of our world (what I'll later call the **"Digital realm"**), one of the foundations of the modern human world.

The question is not "when should we use Digital and when shouldn't we?" or "how do we use Digital correctly to play to its strengths and cover its weaknesses?" Digital is not something you can shed, nor a tool you can take or leave. Digital is the reality we live through. Resisting it is wasted effort. What we should be thinking about is how to coexist with it, how to get along with it.

This section is just about Analog.

Digital is intensely rational, fond of sorting things into categories. Analog is the opposite. So in the previous section I could carefully sort Digital into kinds, give it definitions and readings, explain what Digital is entirely through my own rational telling. The same method will not work on Analog.

Ah, how am I supposed to describe Analog to you?

This is the world under a poet's pen. It is the world of [sensibility](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/_Ciyegz40rpAySjDo5qb7Q), the world [steeped in the scent of coffee](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/tzGUbdXeECx_6bM5HfzUsg).

Analog is literary. It has a literary logic, a logic built on metaphor and feeling, and it does not depend on the logic of Digital. I see someone unhappy and wonder whether I've offended them. That is reasoning built on logic, but it has nothing to do with the airtight logic of Digital.

We can find hidden links and resonances between unrelated things. We can sit with a texture, a mood, a scene, an experience, a single autumn leaf, a single grain of sunlight, and feel each one in detail. That is Analog. Words and feeling are not two things you can pull apart. Our speech carries our private sensations and impressions, and the expression of those sensations and impressions has to pass through speech.

In the broadest sense, "the world" is Analog, because everything we feel about the world around us is "a mapping from one continuous change (the world inside and outside us) to another continuous change (the feeling in our hearts)."

So, just like Digital, Analog is also a dimension of the world, and both alike are the reality we live through.

---

So, on the basis of Analog versus Digital, we can split the symbolic realm in two. I'll call them the **"realm of feeling and speech"** and the **"Digital realm."**

[The symbolic realm's encroachment on the real, the specter of the real tearing open the symbolic.](https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/ZukLQ9nUNgi853Qdb2EIkw) That entanglement of the real and the symbolic was the source of the destructive forces in older societies. But this "imaginary, symbolic, real" framework cannot hold the human world of the information age. The theoretical horizons of post-humanism and post-humanist thought have to be let in.

Humanity in the information age faces a second layer of entanglement, which shows up as **"the Digital realm's encroachment on the realm of feeling and speech, the specter of the realm of feeling and speech tearing open the Digital realm."**

The **"realm of feeling and speech"** is built on metaphor. Our impressions of the real world are abstracted into symbols and words, and through language the reader, drawing on their own experience, "experiences" the experience that someone else's language points toward. So this is a world with a thousand faces for a thousand people, fluid, undecided.

The **"Digital realm"** erases that fluidity and that undecidedness. The digital world is built on formal systems. Binary storage is discrete, of finite precision, so it means quantification, simulation, manipulation, management. The infinitely precise, fully detailed realm of the real, and the shimmering, wholly subjective realm of feeling and speech, neither can be quantified or computed.

> ...In that Empire, the Art of Cartography attained such Perfection that the map of a single Province occupied the entirety of a City, and the map of the Empire, the entirety of a Province.
>
> In time, those Unconscionable Maps no longer satisfied, and the Cartographers Guilds struck a Map of the Empire whose size was that of the Empire, and which coincided point for point with it.
>
> The following Generations, who were not so fond of the Study of Cartography as their Forebears had been, saw that that vast Map was Useless, and not without some Pitilessness was it, that they delivered it up to the Inclemencies of Sun and Winters.
>
> In the Deserts of the West, still today, there are Tattered Ruins of that Map, inhabited by Animals and Beggars; in all the Land there is no other Relic of the Disciplines of Geography.
>
> (complete text)
>
> Borges, *On Exactitude in Science*

The Digital realm is a "map." There is a mountain in the world, and on the map it becomes a triangle symbol or a contour drawing, with a number written beside it to mark the height. I'd ask the reader to notice that the point of this symbolization is not "a symbol standing in for a real thing" (that is the symbolic realm encroaching on the real). The point is the process of becoming a number, which turns a mountain in the real world into something quantifiable, something computable. However fine the map, it is still wildly blurred and distorted next to the real terrain, but precisely because of that, digitalization can give everything a measure. And so this digital world becomes quantifiable, measurable, modelable, computable.

The Digital realm is not a world that the information age invented. Its history reaches back to the very instant humans first measured the scale of the world with a number.

> The true system, the real system, is our present construction of systematic thought itself, rationality itself, and if a factory is torn down but the rationality which produced it is left standing, then that rationality will simply produce another factory. If a revolution destroys a government, but the systematic patterns of thought that produced that government are left intact, then those patterns will repeat themselves... There's so much talk about the system. And so little understanding.
>
> That's all the motorcycle is, a system of concepts worked out in steel. There's no part in it, no shape in it, that is not out of someone's mind... Steel? Hell, even the steel is out of someone's mind. There's no steel in nature. Anyone from the Bronze Age could have told you that. All nature has is a potential for steel. There's nothing else there. But what's "potential"? That's also in someone's mind!... Ghosts.
>
> Pirsig, *Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance*

The Digital realm closely resembles the symbolic realm, but the Digital realm permits no ambiguity. The Digital realm is **"formal."**

By "formal" I mean a thing does not depend on its carrier. The nature of a thing is settled by some relation of quantity. Everything is built into a harmonious order according to certain numerical proportions, and yet the material carrier the thing rests on does not matter.

A video game uses computer graphics to draw mountains, rivers, grass, and trees. What we see on the screen is the rendered two-dimensional image, while all the model entities (the matter) and the relations and interactions between entities (the physics) are stored and recorded in the form of data.

If the precision of the model entities were pushed to the limit, they would be no different from the matter of the real world. Push the precision of the relations and interactions between entities to the limit, and the matter is given physical laws no different from the real world's. The essence of a computer program is a stretch of data, a string of numbers. A computer program is "formal." Whether it runs on transistors or vacuum tubes, the abstract program is the same, and it runs.

Industry has the concept of the **"digital twin."** A digital twin reproduces, in virtual space, every parameter and metric of a physical object, so it mirrors the whole life cycle of the corresponding piece of equipment. A digital twin is not just a model or a simulation. The digital twin is the object itself. The matter and the carrier they rest on differ, but from the standpoint of form, there is no true and false dividing material elements from matrix data. The object is no more real than the digital twin, and the digital twin is no more false than the object.

The world of the future is arriving far earlier than you imagine. The smartphone has long stopped being just a "tool" for humans and is slowly becoming an "organ." In science fiction, the human brain can be wired to external storage to expand its memory.

In fact, that wish has already been partly granted. For people in the habit of jotting things down, the phone has already become the brain's external hard drive. Instant messaging and social media have encroached on traditional human relationships and ways of talking. When you see someone on the street, a Bluetooth earpiece in, talking with a far-off friend, you can feel the tension of a future about to arrive.

In the post-pandemic era, the line between the virtual world and the real one has blurred. During lockdown, life in the online space was more real than life in the flesh.

In that sense, the cyborg (the fused system of human and electronic machine) is not what we are about to become. It is what we have already become.

---

In mathematics, the natural sciences, the factory, and the program, Digital is natural, harmless, correct. The Digital realm's encroachment on the symbolic realm does not happen here, because in these settings the symbols were already formalized to begin with (an electron is an electron, with no room to be misread). The Digital realm and the symbolic realm are identical, so naturally there is no rupture.

The trouble with the "Digital realm" is that in this tidy, complete picture, there is no place to hold our joy and our pain, no place for our moral longings and our artistic ideals.

That last paragraph is not mine. It is Professor Chen Jiaying's criticism of the "scientific world," and the criticism applies just as well to the "Digital realm." The "Digital realm" is larger than "the world that science paints." The world of science is far from our daily lives, but the Digital realm is already part of them.

Digitalization cares about the general structures and laws across different things, about binary qualities and hierarchical structures. It leans toward analysis.

When that line of thinking cuts into the complex psychological states and emotional experiences of human beings, into our imaginings of a good society and a just politics, into literature, philosophy, and dreams, a second alienation and distortion of the person begins.

In *37: A Vast Resemblance Linking All Things*, Baoting mentions a phrase:

**"the Digital Singularity"**

The "Digital Singularity" is the instant when everything in the world can be quantified and digitized, the instant when information technology is everywhere and has fused with our lives.

By then the territory of the "Digital realm" will cover the whole human world. There will be nothing that cannot be digitized.

The Digital Singularity is arriving earlier than you imagine.

Do you remember the last time you used cash in renminbi?

I can't anymore. The last time I dug a coin out of my little change pouch, it felt utterly foreign. Somewhere along the way cash in China became a nostalgic thing, a thing with a faint whiff of the exotic.

Back before the pandemic, when you could still travel abroad, I always liked to bring home some leftover foreign currency I couldn't spend, as a souvenir. Now the renminbi has become that kind of thing for me too. I think it's pretty, I like to carry a little on me, and yet I'm not sure how to use it, how to actually spend it.

I walk into a supermarket and everything is priced at something-point-99. If I paid in cash, how would the cashier even give me change? I don't know. But just picturing the scene makes me want to laugh. Cash really does seem to belong to the last era.

I once saw a joke on Douban. The poster went to a convenience store to buy ice cream, happened to have cash on them, and wanted to seize the chance to use it. They asked the owner, "Boss, do you take spirit money here?" The owner froze on the spot, and only then did it hit the poster: "Ah, I mean cash. Do you take cash?"

Even the word "cash" has grown that strange. Kids today see a floppy disk (maybe you don't know what a floppy disk is either, here, it's this: 💾) and ask, curious, "Hello, is this a physical model of the 'save button'?"

Maybe the kids of the future will see cash and ask, "Hello, is this thing 'physical money'?"

All of this happened in the span of just twenty years.

In 2004, Alipay split off from Taobao to become a third-party payment platform.

In 2013, WeChat Pay burst onto the scene.

It started with e-commerce platforms needing electronic payment. Then supermarkets and convenience stores took up mobile payment too. Now even the street vendor selling grilled sausages needs to stick a payment code on the grill, or plenty of customers won't know how to pay.

The "Digital Singularity" of payment has already happened.

Can the Digital Singularity in other domains be far behind?

It makes me think of the dessert plate at the end of a meal. A waiter walks over and says, "value's up~", and the diners chat and laugh a while, someone leaves the table to settle the bill, and at last everyone heads off happy.

I'm sorry. In this piece, at the close of *Re-Understanding the World*, I can't serve you that plate of "value."

Analog and Digital, the "Digital realm" and the "realm of symbol and feeling," so many good and bad things are tangled together. I've done what I can in these pages to sort it out for you, to lay it out point by point, but looking back at the two essays, I still feel I've written it messy and roundabout. You cut and it won't sever, you smooth it and it tangles again.

**So, what is there for us to do?**

Or to put it another way: having thought all of this through, how should we get along with this world, and how should we get along with ourselves?

This is an open question. I have no standing to give you any settled answer.

But I can tell you about a new hobby I've suddenly picked up lately: watching the clouds and the sky.

Generally a clear day is dull, even a little kitsch. Blue sky, drifting white clouds, sunlight spilling everywhere. Cute enough at first glance, ordinary once you've seen it often.

But even with nothing but bare blue sky, I seem to have slowly learned to find some pleasure in it. The sky isn't always blue. It can be other colors too. And even when it is blue, every day can be a different blue, the color shifting by the hour.

The sky out the window of an empty meeting room looks one way. The sky through a bright glass curtain wall looks another.

Even a dirty pane of glass or a pair of fogged-up glasses puts a different filter over the sky.

You can watch the ground too. Shenzhen sits at a low latitude, and now and then at noon you can see the sunlight come straight down, the shadows of people and buildings falling small at their feet, so the world around you takes on a faint sense of unreality.

You can look at the buildings, too. Now that I've left school, none of the buildings in view are the work of any great architect, but the way light lands on concrete walls and glass windows, that texture, shifts with my own steps and with the passing of time. The scene changes as you walk, and it's lovely, though the phone camera can't capture it at all.

Watching a film in a cinema is not the same as watching it on a laptop. Seeing a show in person is not the same as looking at photos of the art. Chatting on WeChat is not the same as chatting in a café. Hearing a live concert is not the same as listening to lossless audio through earphones.

Hold on to life. Feel it. Don't let it slip away.
