The seed of this essay was planted a long time ago. Lately I have had a little spare time, so I have finally sat down to write it out.

The original idea was to braid together posthumanist theory with the thinking of Lacan and Žižek, fold in a notion I call the "Digital order," and from all of that sketch a fuller picture of the world we live in now and the one that is almost here.

But the more I wrote, the more the word count swelled. What started as a memo of a hundred or so characters, even when I stuck to the essentials, slowly grew into something closer to a small philosophical treatise of nearly ten thousand words.

So I figured I might as well take the chance to put some of my underlying theoretical logic down on paper, and to share it with more people while I am at it.

---

Lacan and Žižek divide the subject into three orders, or dimensions:

- the Imaginary (Imaginary Order)
- the Symbolic (Symbolic Order)
- the Real (The Real)

---

A human subject's knowledge of itself is always indirect. You cannot know yourself directly. You have to come to know yourself through the feedback of your interactions with the outside world.

We look at ourselves in mirrors and on camera to learn what our own face looks like. We build our sense of who we are now and who we ideally want to be out of external systems of judgment and the moral exemplars around us.

And so a person takes the scrutinized, objectified self for the real self. This **misrecognition** is what builds our self-knowledge, and because it is rooted deep in our minds, this is the **Imaginary**.

---

The **Symbolic**, or the order of signs, is a world built entirely by human beings.

A symbol, a sign, is language in the broad sense, and the discussion of signs and language has been at the center of modern philosophy. Saussure's structuralist linguistics tells us that a sign is made of two parts: the **signifier** and the **signified**.

The signifier is the sign's image or sound shape. The signified is the concept being expressed.

The signifier lives in the Symbolic. The signified lives in the real world.

The classic example is the following statement:

> **"Snow is white"** is true if and only if **snow is white**.

The first half is the signifier, the marks on the page or the sounds in the air. The second half is the signified, the snow out in the real world actually showing up as the color white.

A great deal of what later philosophers and sociologists wrote rests on reading and interpreting signs. Take Baudrillard's critique in *The Consumer Society*: the consumption of objects no longer stops at use value but turns on sign value. To be turned into a sign is to be turned into a commodity, and commodity-signs form an endless chain of signs and meanings. Body, experience, relationships, status, all of it gets converted into signs and folded into the order of commodities.

**The Symbolic is false, and yet it is not false.** It contains the whole of "the real as confirmed by signs."

J. L. Austin, of the ordinary language school, gave us the influential theory of the **speech act**: some kinds of speaking are equivalent to the act itself, and the act in turn has effects on the world.

Marriage, for instance, is a wholly verbal process. To pronounce a marriage is to marry. Nothing in the real world changes, and yet the social relationship between two people, and the rest of their lives, will never be the same. This is because the Symbolic, though it has nothing to do with the real world, is the world we have been forced to inhabit ever since we acquired self-consciousness and the capacity for language. Social relations, nations and peoples, property rights and human rights, language and signs, all of these exist, and exist only, within the Symbolic.

If you want to understand why something like a nation or a society also exists only within the Symbolic, go read *Imagined Communities*. I will not belabor the point here.

---

Now for the **Real**, the hardest and most exquisite part of Lacan's and Žižek's thought.

In fact you have already met the Real in the pages above. It is that "real world" I kept mentioning. The Real is the truth of the world, the chaotic world before it has been carved up by signs and cognition.

Hayek held that the basic function of the brain is to classify things. To classify is to give things names so as to mark off the differences between one thing and another, which is to say it is the process of turning the world into signs. But between one thing and another there is no boundary to begin with. The boundary is something we made, and it lives in the Symbolic.

When I see an animal's tracks in the snow, I will say those are "footprints." But to a small insect, they are not "footprints." They are "the rise and fall of the terrain." Our knowledge of the world comes with a built-in understanding of the world, which is why understanding the world as another person or another creature sees it is harder than you would think.

Of course, "terrain" and "rise and fall" are not good enough phrases either, because these words are concepts within the human language system. They already carry our basic cognitive model of things, and that model is just as absent from the insect's mind.

What this example shows us is that the **Real** cannot be felt or known, because any act of knowing is a process of turning things into signs and structures, and that process belongs to the Symbolic, not to the Real.

Seen through an evolutionary lens, a system of signs or a body of concepts arises from an organism adapting to the rewards and punishments of its surroundings. So the organism's environment, in turn, sets the use and meaning of its signs. This sits comfortably with Wittgenstein's line that to imagine a language is to imagine a form of life.

**The Real is simply the world before it is known.** To borrow Heidegger's account of Being in *Being and Time*, the Real is the world with *Dasein* absent. To put it in a rough metaphor: the real world is itself gray and dim, and the presence of a conscious subject lights it up. But the world that gets observed is not exactly the same as the world before it was observed. The presence of the observing consciousness, that is to say "I," that is *Dasein*, can never be ignored.

---

The Real is quite close to the physical world that modern science has disenchanted: no nations or societies running their machinery, no human joy or sorrow, only the mechanical universe of elementary particles in motion.

But the Real is more chaotic, more primordial, than the world the physicist sees, because signs like "electron" and "photon" are not even allowed there. Not even the laws of mathematics make it in, since mathematics is a formal language.

**"Theory-ladenness of observation"** is a famous thesis in the philosophy of science. Observation has to rest on theory. The method, the object, the purpose of an observation all have a conceptual scaffolding behind them, so theory seeps into observation and changes its results. That makes **"objective observation"** impossible.

To run a physics experiment, I necessarily need some premise and intention that lets the experiment happen at all, and that intention soaks into my experiment. So there is no such thing as a detached, objective experimental result. Which is to say, in the strictest sense, science does not transcend experience.

Theory-ladenness also exposes a more frightening fact: there is no objective history, which is to say history as a discipline is impossible from the inside. Croce said **"all history is contemporary history,"** and Benjamin Schwartz reopened his book *The World of Thought in Ancient China* by restating the same idea: **"the history of the past is inescapably always the history of the present."** This is a return to humane concern and a care for the problems of our own age, and it is also the historian flinching away from the problem of the Real.

In science we can see a similar flinch. Thomas Kuhn, in *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*, gave us the widely known concept of the **"paradigm."** A paradigm is rather like a "namespace" in a programming language. A scientific conclusion is always a conclusion under a given paradigm, and it is correct within the bounds of that paradigm. There is no denying the value of the **"paradigm"** as a guide to research, but the incommensurability between paradigms became a sore point with no easy cure. When asked whether relativity overturned Newtonian mechanics, Kuhn's answer was that the two understand and define space and time differently, so each is correct within its own paradigm. **There is nothing wrong with this point as far as it goes, but it offers no guidance on how to reconcile or mediate the differences between paradigms, and it never confronts the subtle influence that human cognition and human vocabulary have on scientific research.** (Newton and Einstein may define space and time differently, but the words they use are the same.)

In 1972, Philip Anderson, a founder of condensed matter physics and a Nobel laureate, published a half-philosophical paper in *Nature* titled *More is Different*, which dug deep into the hierarchical structure of science.

Elementary particle physics, condensed matter physics, chemistry, molecular biology, cell biology, physiology, psychology, the social sciences: across these levels, the entities at each level have to obey the laws of the level above, and yet at every level we have to find new concepts, definitions, laws, and principles. Those principles cannot simply be deduced from the level above, because a change in scale brings about a qualitative change in theory. This phenomenon is now widely called **"emergence."**

**From a naive reductionist standpoint, if we understood the properties of the most basic matter, then the more macroscopic properties should be "derivable."** But in practice, bridging the gaps between the disciplines at different levels has become one of modern science's hard problems. We have gained powerful tools like statistical mechanics and network science, yet the theoretical chasm between the disciplines is far from filled.

Hayek, the Nobel economist, and Qian Xuesen, whom many of us know well, both turned their late scholarly interest toward complex systems theory. I suspect they both sensed the same cloud that hangs over every theory: the very nature of the Real means it cannot be structured or turned into signs in any form whatsoever, and **the attempt to make it into signs often leaves behind a remainder that cannot be made into signs.** This shows up as Arrow's impossibility theorem in voting theory, Gödel's incompleteness theorems in mathematics, and emergent properties in complexity science.

A small preview: from a more contemporary vantage, these striking "impossibilities" cannot be credited entirely to **"the rift of the Real within the Symbolic."** They are also partly due to **"the Digital order's encroachment on the order of feeling and speech."**

Did two new terms just startling you by appearing out of nowhere? Don't worry. The problem of the Digital order is something we will unpack slowly in a later essay.

---

The Welsh poet R. S. Thomas wrote, in his poem *Reflections*:

> The furies are at home in the mirror; it is their address.
>
> Even the clearest water, if deep enough, can drown.
>
> from R. S. Thomas, *Reflections*

The **"furies"** in the poem are the ghost of the Real. They are the **"incompleteness"** internal to the Symbolic. The subject of the Real is encroached upon and murdered by the sign-bound identity of the Symbolic, and this murdered subject becomes a vengeful ghost, tearing rift after rift in the Symbolic.

To borrow a line from Guy Debord's *The Society of the Spectacle*: **"In a world that has really been turned upside down, the true is a moment of the false."** That inverted world is the Symbolic, and the truth of the Real can only show itself in the instant when the Symbolic contradicts itself.

(A small note: my "borrowing" of Guy Debord above is really an instance of what his Situationist theory calls *détournement*. The concept of the spectacle is itself a *détournement* of capital from Marx's *Capital*, and my reading of Debord above is a second *détournement*.)

Camus caught this beautifully in his work and called it the **"absurd."** The sense of the absurd comes precisely from the incompleteness of the symbolic order. When a person, as an actor, feels estranged from the stage of the world, they feel that absurdity.

> Seen in the fullest light, even war and heroes contain a contradiction.
>
> A nation can throw itself into war only after it has found the other side it agrees to be in conflict with, and so each side is necessarily entangled with the other in complicated ways:
>
> Before I can have an enemy, I must persuade this person to regard me as an enemy.
>
> I cannot become a hero unless I first find someone who will threaten my life, even take it.
>
> from James Carse, *Finite and Infinite Games*

**The Symbolic is theatrical.** Every concept has to depend on its counter-concept. In a concrete context, a word has a precise, palpable meaning, but on the abstract field of discourse, a word is forced to lean on the concept that opposes it.

The slogan of "justice" comes out of hatred for and rebellion against "injustice." But if there were no justice and no injustice in the world to begin with, then the pursuit of "justice" would naturally collapse into the absurd.

In Genesis, the Lord warns the first man, Adam: **"But of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, thou shalt not eat of it, for in the day that thou eatest thereof thou shalt surely die."** The forbidden fruit that Adam and Eve ate was this fruit of **the knowledge of good and evil**.

The Real has neither good nor evil. The division into good and evil belongs to the Symbolic. Following this line of reading, the humans who can tell good from evil (the Symbolic) are naturally cast out of the whole and harmonious Eden (the Real) and sentenced to exile. And the "surely die" is the death of humanity's subject in the Real. **We cannot face the chaotic and real Real head on. We can only live in the theatrical Symbolic.**
