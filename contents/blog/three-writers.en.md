> Life can't do without literature,
>
> and literature can't do without the avant-garde.

I like to call myself an Indie Fetish.

It's a phrase I made up. You could roughly translate it as "lover of all things independent."

The word "indie" comes out of video games. There's still no clean definition of an "indie game," but mostly it means a game made in an independent spirit: the creator is brave enough to express themselves, to show ideas and inventions that look nothing like the rest, and the whole point is the expression rather than the profit.

People who make indie games are dreamers. They have no backing from a studio or a publisher, so they have to carry the entire cost of development themselves. In exchange they get the one thing every creator dreams of: total creative freedom. And that freedom is exactly what gives us so many strange, wonderful, one-of-a-kind works.

I'm forever chasing things with that "independent streak," the way a pilgrim chases a shrine.

Avant-garde literature, contemporary art, experimental music, art-house cinema, indie games.

Brilliant creators smash the old rules. They go after new ways of telling a story, new forms, new rhythms and timbres, new camera moves, new ways of letting you interact.

And their breakthroughs become the new templates, which the next generation then studies, imitates, and overturns.

As long as the independent spirit keeps going, the wheel of culture keeps turning.

But that's a tangent for another day. Today I want to talk about literature made in this independent spirit.

I don't really like sorting literature into categories. Every writer has a temperament all their own, and every classic has its own private cleverness. Try to pin a piece of writing into some rigid taxonomy and you smother the very thing that makes it fun, drowning it under one stuffy "-ism" or another.

So, the way music critics toss anything hard to define into "alternative," I'm going to wave my hand and dump all these independent-minded works into one big bucket labeled "avant-garde literature." Bear with me, reader.

Three writers I love a great deal:

- **Professor Cervantes**

  The titan who killed off the chivalric romance and invented both the metafiction and the modern novel. Compiler of the Spanish joke book.

- **Professor Borges**

  A philosopher-writer who was blind and yet saw clear to the far shore. He left behind so much good prose and so many good poems you'll never get through them all.

- **Professor Calvino**

  Famous for an astonishing trilogy of novels, who then fell so deep into writing postmodern fiction he couldn't climb back out.

## Cervantes

Every literary critic loves Cervantes's *Don Quixote*. The scholarship around the book and its author has become a discipline of its own.

It seems impossible to praise *Don Quixote* too highly, and you'll find it on every single "must-read" list.

But I'll say this: a classic is a classic, and still, where you read *Don Quixote* matters.

Study hall, the library, sneaking a read during class. None of these are good places for *Don Quixote*.

Because doubling over laughing is a bit rude, and clamping your hand over your mouth to stifle it is a good way to give yourself a stitch.

The story goes that Philip III of Spain, looking down from a palace balcony, saw a student reading and roaring with laughter, and said the boy was either reading *Don Quixote* or out of his mind. Sure enough, the student was reading *Don Quixote*.

Most Chinese essays analyzing *Don Quixote* fix on the qualities of the hero, the pitiable, ridiculous, admirable knight Don Quixote himself, but you rarely see anything written about how radical the book's storytelling actually was.

At the start of Part Two, Don Quixote hears that his deeds have been written up into a biography (that biography being Part One of *Don Quixote*), and that it has spread far and wide. Curious how the biographer has portrayed him, he calls in a young scholar to talk the book over.

And so we get one of the scenes that earned its place in literary history:

Don Quixote and a young scholar sit around sizing up *Don Quixote*, picking it apart. Don Quixote thinks the book should have written less about the times he got beaten up; the scholar points out that one of the inserted stories doesn't fit and has nothing to do with the knight's tale.

> ...he told me my history is in a book called *The Ingenious Gentleman Don Quixote of La Mancha*. He says I'm in it too, by my own name, Sancho Panza, and so is the lady Dulcinea del Toboso, and other things that only the two of us went through, so that I crossed myself in sheer wonder, unable to grasp how the man who wrote it could have known.
>
> From *Don Quixote*, Cervantes

*Don Quixote* isn't only the first "modern novel" in literary history. It's also the first work of metafiction.

"Metafiction" is fiction about fiction. To borrow the definition from Patricia Waugh, professor of English at Durham University:

"Metafiction is a term given to fictional writing which self-consciously and systematically draws attention to its status as an artefact in order to pose questions about the relationship between fiction and reality."

In a traditional work, the world of the book and the world of the reader are sealed off from each other. The reader is a bystander who, by quiet agreement, never doubts that the book's world is real, never interacts with anything inside it, and just sits and watches the story unfold. Metafiction is different. It tries to break the fourth wall: pulling the reader into the book as one of the characters, or deliberately making the reader feel the fiction as fiction, or letting the characters realize that they themselves are made up.

And just like that, the line between the imagined and the real comes down.

Think of Marvel's *Deadpool*, where our antihero figures out he's a character in a comic and slaughters the entire universe just to flip off the author and the readers on the outside.

Or the game *Undertale*, where the characters know the protagonist is the player, the one with the power to "save" and "reset." Once you reach the perfect ending, the game even begs you not to "reset" this world, to let it finally rest in peace.

Honestly, the clever narrative tricks in *Don Quixote* go far beyond this (at one point a character grumbles, on the author's behalf, about his own missed deadlines on an earlier piece). I'd written pages quoting and unpacking it all, then thought better of it and cut the whole thing. No need to spoil more than I have. It would be a shame to ruin the strangeness and delight of your first read.

## Borges

> What can I hold you with?
>
> I offer you lean streets, desperate sunsets, the moon of the ragged suburbs.
>
> I offer you the bitterness of a man who has looked long and long at the lonely moon.
>
> From "Two English Poems," Borges

Borges is much loved in China. His prose and his poetry are both superb. Not just good, but vast: the *Collected Works of Borges* has to be split into a first volume and a second, two thick stacks that together run to nearly a thousand yuan, and you still can't read it all.

Borges loved Cervantes. He has a famous piece, "Pierre Menard, Author of the *Quixote*," that tells this story:

A writer, Pierre Menard, decides to rewrite *Don Quixote* in the twentieth century, word for word, line for line, so that it coincides exactly with the original. For convenience, let's call Cervantes's work *Don Quixote* and Menard's work the *Quixote*. The distinction is necessary, even though the two are identical.

The very same sentence means different things in the *Quixote* and in *Don Quixote*. Under Cervantes's hand a line is "a rhetorical praise of history" and "a fluent handling of the Spanish then current," while under Menard's hand the same line becomes "brazen pragmatism" and "an affected, archaizing style."

The first time I read "Pierre Menard, Author of the *Quixote*," I took it for a harmless little joke of Borges's, a tribute to the great Cervantes built out of something close to sophistry.

But when I went back and read it closely, Borges won me over. I started to believe, from the bottom of my heart, that if the *Quixote* really existed, it would in fact be richer than *Don Quixote*. Even if every single word were the same, I would genuinely take them to be two different works.

To borrow Borges's own phrase from the piece, this is a new technique: deliberately scrambling the dates and the attributions, and in doing so changing the way a book gets read.

It's like reading the *Odyssey* as though it came after the *Iliad*, or reading Lu Xun's *Call to Arms* as if it were a present-day microblog post, so that the work shakes loose from the assumptions built into its author and its era, and gains an endless capacity to be interpreted across the whole vast field of possible authors and times.

If the metanarrative of *Don Quixote* blurs the false and the true, teasing and bewildering the reader, then "Pierre Menard, Author of the *Quixote*" teaches the reader how to make a real text turn unreal, throwing the author into disorder so that the calmest book brims with surprise.

That's the kind of writer Borges is. The more you turn him over in your mind, the deeper he gets.

A short passage from his "The Analytical Language of John Wilkins" became the original spark for *The Order of Things*, by the contemporary French philosopher Michel Foucault.

Borges is so distinctive that once you've read him, the moment you come across a new piece you can tell almost at once it's his. Very few people can imitate his style, but Chen Chuncheng's *The Submarine in the Night* manages it. If you love Borges, I'd recommend you read *The Submarine in the Night* too.

## Calvino

Have you ever heard of that hotly debated perfect-score essay from the college entrance exam, "Living in the Trees"?

Its title comes straight from Calvino's novel *The Baron in the Trees*, whose hero is the eldest son of a noble family in the 18th and 19th centuries. After one quarrel with his family, he goes to live in the trees and refuses ever to come back down to the ground.

Yes, Calvino is famous for his trilogy *Our Ancestors* (*The Cloven Viscount*, *The Baron in the Trees*, *The Nonexistent Knight*), but my own favorites are his later postmodern novels, *Invisible Cities* and *If on a winter's night a traveler*.

This essay will only talk about *If on a winter's night a traveler*. Take one look at the first paragraph of the first chapter and you'll see why I had no choice but to bring it up.

> You are about to begin reading Italo Calvino's new novel, *If on a winter's night a traveler*. Relax. Concentrate. Dispel every other thought. Let the world around you fade. Best to close the door; the TV is always on in the next room. Tell the others right away, "No, I don't want to watch TV!" Raise your voice or they won't hear you. "I'm reading! I don't want to be disturbed!" Maybe they haven't heard you, with all that racket; speak louder, yell, "I'm beginning to read Italo Calvino's new novel!" Or if you prefer, don't say anything; just hope they'll leave you alone.
>
> From *If on a winter's night a traveler*, Calvino

I have to say, when it comes to playing with how a story is told and what form it takes, *If on a winter's night a traveler* reaches a height almost no one else can touch.

Once, when I was reading some of the narrative-bending pieces on the SCP Foundation (an online collaborative-fiction community), I was floored. In the stories from the Narrative Department, humans realize they're inside a story and try to seize control of the writing. In the Metaphysics stories, humans discover their fate is governed by a god, the "horror writer," and try to kill that god to win back their own peace and freedom. In the antimemetics stories, the very thing a piece describes erases the information about itself, so the writing comes off as vague and evasive, and the text turns into an elegant little puzzle box, where the blank space itself becomes a strange and seductive part of the reading.

A new medium gives birth to a new literature. Writing on the web can lean hard on hyperlinks and wiki syntax, so creators can write with entirely new methods and formats. As I keep insisting, format and medium are part of the content too. You could even say the medium is the most essential thing, and the words are just how the medium shows itself. Different media give rise to different literatures, and a brand-new medium gives rise to a brand-new one.

The Foundation sits right at the leading edge of avant-garde writing. As the community's tastes shifted, it long ago broke free of the urban-legend cage and became one of the most inventive and open-ended literary communities around, even producing a few works you could fairly call serious literature.

But playing with form and narrative isn't some fresh thing that only appeared once the internet gave us online literature in the 21st century. As pleasing as the Foundation writers' achievements are, set them next to Calvino's *If on a winter's night a traveler* and they're still a long way short. Here are two passages, picked at random:

> The novel begins in a railway station, a locomotive huffing, steam from a piston covering the opening of the chapter, a cloud of smoke hiding part of the first paragraph. ...
>
> I am the man who comes and goes between the bar and the telephone booth. Or rather: that man is called "I" and you know nothing else about him, just as this station is called only "the station" and beyond it there exists nothing except the unanswered signal of a telephone ringing in a dark room of a distant city. ...
>
> From *If on a winter's night a traveler*, Calvino

Writing tricks like these fill this not-exactly-thin book. Every time I read *If on a winter's night a traveler* there's some surprise waiting for me, and now and then it pulls a "wait, you can write like that?" right out of me.

To put it a little more romantically: some of the Foundation's pieces are like dazzling stars, throwing off a light that makes you sit up. Calvino's writing is the whole galaxy. To call it the slow accumulation of mastery doesn't go far enough; it's something you can only look up to.

## Hypertext and metaliterature

In 1960 Ted Nelson conceived of a way to handle text on a computer and called it "hypertext." The idea later fed into the HTTP protocol we all know; the HT in HTTP is Hyper Text. Hypertext uses hyperlinks to bind together text from different spaces into a single web.

Milorad Pavić's *Dictionary of the Khazars* and Raymond Queneau's *Hundred Thousand Billion Poems* might both belong in the category of the "hypertext novel."

The first is a dictionary of an imaginary world, where the entire setting and all its stories have been broken apart and tucked into individual word entries, left for the reader to track down. The second is a poetry collection meant to be combined at will: every line of every page is cut into a separate horizontal strip, so the reader can assemble a hundred thousand billion different sonnets.

Cast the net wider and the game *Disco Elysium* counts as a hypertext novel too. You play a detective, and out of countless choices you steer where the story goes, while the red revolution, the founding myths, self-redemption, and political reflection all lie buried deep in the text. The games *Dark Souls* and *Elden Ring* count as well. The legendary creator Hidetaka Miyazaki shattered the whole world and its history into fragments and hid them in item descriptions and crumbling ruins, and there are players online who reconstruct the rise and fall of dynasties, the loves and hatreds of a dying land, purely from forensic study of the game's statues, family crests, and architecture.

So what does "hypertext" have to do with the theme of this essay?

Borges wrote "Pierre Menard, Author of the *Quixote*," which points back to Cervantes; and Calvino, in his *Why Read the Classics?*, lavishes praise on Borges.

Maybe it's just my own narrow view and bias, but I have this faint sense that some mysterious line of succession runs between these three writers, that this chain of pointing forms a peculiar "hyperlink." Each carries his own differently flavored "independent spirit," and one generation after another they break through the old forms of literature, searching for its new possibilities, then becoming the new form themselves, to be studied, imitated, and broken through by those who come after.

I think this might be a metaphor for the whole history of literature, and beyond that for the whole history of thought, the whole history of humankind.

The philosopher Heidegger doesn't put the human being at the center of the world. He sees the human as standing within the whole web of the world's connections, and he calls that whole the "fourfold."

The existence and meaning of everything is linked to everything else, forming a "web of meaning" across four-dimensional space and time, and every question about "meaning" has to find its answer somewhere in that web. A classic example: what is the meaning of a hammer? Examine it in isolation, on a single slice of space and time, and it's nothing but a stick of wood joined to a lump of iron, with no "meaning" at all. Only by placing it within the whole of time, from the craftsman who made the hammer from nothing for some purpose, to the person who uses it to drive a nail, can the hammer's meaning be sensed and understood.

The meaning of a life works the same way, as the philosopher Chen Jiaying puts it in *The Reasons of Value*: "I and the people and things around me fuse into one inextricable fate." We can't fully cut ourselves off from the world around us. We have to care about it, to be concerned with it, and it's that care that keeps us, as beings, connected to the world.

> Because the nature of Dasein lies in its existence, that is, in the realizing of its possibilities. Any movement from the present reality toward a future state inevitably gives rise to the question: **"What shall I do?"** This is Care. Care is rooted in Dasein's capacity to choose its own being.
>
> Care is regarded as the fundamental relation between Dasein and the world, and the ground on which Dasein attains meaning in this world. It is the basic condition of all of Dasein's experience.
>
> From Wikipedia, "Martin Heidegger"

He badly wanted to say: the fourfold is hypertext and metafiction, that countless links make up the "web," and that history and meaning are born from it.

But he stared at the cursor blinking on the screen for a long while, and for the moment had no idea what to write next.

It was time for this essay to reach its close, and Ning Ning Ning Jing Hai had no idea how to how to end it.

After writing four lines with a meta touch to them, he had a sudden flash of inspiration and decided to end with a poem by Bei Dao.

It's the shortest poem in the Chinese language. The whole thing is a single character:

> Net
>
> From "Life," Bei Dao

I'm done.
