## Cognitive science EP1, an introduction: what is cognitive science?

Cognitive science is a frontier, cross-disciplinary field that studies how the human brain, or the mind, actually works. It is a fairly young one.

Back in 1975, scholars brought together six fields, **philosophy**, **psychology**, **linguistics**, **anthropology**, **computer science**, and **neuroscience**, to ask a single question: how is information passed along during the act of knowing? That research program gave rise to a new discipline, cognitive science. It is young, and yet it has already made an indelible contribution to one of the largest mysteries in the universe, the question of how the brain works.

"How do humans think? How do we come to know the world?" This is one of the oldest and hardest questions we have ever asked ourselves.

* **The philosopher**

  Philosophers care about the nature and the boundaries of concepts. They turn over questions like: what is "knowledge"? What is "reason"? What is "thinking"?

* **The psychologist**

  Psychologists have both an analytic and an experimental temperament. Some of them ask what the structure of the human mind is. Others study the biological mechanisms of perception.

* **The linguist**

  Linguists noticed that human thought and cognition are inseparable from "language." They ask how we acquire language, and how language shapes the way we think.

* **The anthropologist**

  Anthropologists bring depth in time, along with a stockpile of historical material from peoples and cultures all over the world. They want to know whether cognition is shaped by place and culture, and how the capacity for thought and cognition came into being at all, out of nothing.

* **The computer scientist**

  Computer scientists are practical people who care about getting things to work. They want machines to learn to think, to have intelligence. They study how to process vision with code, and how the algorithm of "thinking" might be written.

* **The neuroscientist**

  If the brain is the organ of thought and the neuron is its medium, then understanding and mapping the workings of the brain and the nervous system becomes essential. Neuroscientists care about the structure of neurons and how they handle signals, about how functions can be localized to regions of the brain, and about what the network of connections among neurons actually looks like.

We all know that what we call an academic discipline is really just a set of research practices built up around one **"question of fundamental importance."** But little by little, researchers in different fields began to realize something. Even though their disciplines worked in different ways, the big question underneath was the same. And to crack the big question of thought and cognition, what we need is not the methods and results of any single discipline, but theories and insights that stretch across many of them at once.

**And so cognitive science was born.** I do not know whether the 21st century will be the century of the life sciences, but I am certain it will be the century of cognitive science.

In fact, it is not only the six pillar fields above. Many more areas of study have joined this cross-disciplinary carnival and overlap deeply with cognitive science.

A few examples, picked almost at random:

* **Behavioral economics**

  Behavioral economics is a currently fashionable sub-branch of economics. It fuses cognitive neuroscience, experimental psychology, and economics in order to find the errors and omissions in today's economic models, and then to repair the shaky assumptions that mainstream economics makes about people: that we are rational, self-interested, fully informed, utility-maximizing, and consistent in our preferences.

* **Neurolaw**

  Neurolaw is a field of legal science that emerged around the 1990s. It mainly uses the research methods and technical tools of cognitive neuroscience to probe the neural activity of the human brain, and then to analyze, interpret, and investigate questions of legal theory and practice.

* **Neuroaesthetics**

  Neuroaesthetics is ~~a notoriously old IELTS reading passage~~ a relatively new sub-branch of experimental aesthetics, grounded in neural perception and artistic creation. It explains and understands aesthetics at the scale of the nervous system, asking whether activity in the same region of the brain is tied to aesthetic experiences that come from very different sources.

**Cognitive science is not merely a "discipline." It is a "paradigm."** As functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) has matured, and as the algorithms and hardware behind artificial intelligence and deep learning have filled in, we have finally found a way to analyze "how humans think and perceive" from the standpoint of science. "Thought and perception" is no longer a purely conceptual problem for philosophy. At last it stands a real chance of being answered well, within the framework of science.

Every time I read a book or a paper in cognitive science, I feel a jolt, the sense that the world has just been turned upside down. Stella, who taught the "Introduction to Cognitive Science" course, used to say that cognitive science is a field where the researcher has to let the mind "run wild." How do we wake up out of sleep? How do we decide what to eat in the dining hall? These are good questions that today's science still cannot answer.

The research here comes from every corner of the map: philosophy, psychology, computation, biology, and on and on. Whatever your background happens to be, your knowledge can spark something new.

This is a place where unsolved problems lie everywhere, and the hunger to know spills out of every corner.

There are no "stupid questions" here. The door is always open to anyone who loves to think, to ask, and to dig.
